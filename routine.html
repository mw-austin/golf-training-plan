Pre-Shot Routine

You said you don’t feel comfortable standing over the ball. That’s not a swing problem — it’s an execution problem. A consistent pre-shot routine is the fix. Built in Phase 3. Used on every shot, forever.

Why this matters

The diagnosis section described the mechanical causes of your misses: early extension, open clubface, over-the-top path. Those are fixable on the range. But a second, separate problem is that you report not feeling comfortable over the ball — and that discomfort produces hesitation, tension, and second-guessing, all of which destroy mechanics that were working fine on the range.

Vision54 coaches Pia Nilsson and Lynn Marriott developed the Think Box / Play Box model specifically to address the mental execution gap between practice and performance. The core insight: make all decisions before you enter your address position, then trust and commit. No thinking allowed in the Play Box.

The single-sentence version: Decide everything behind the ball (Think Box). Step in and swing without second-guessing (Play Box). The moment you step into your address, your job is over and your body’s job begins.

The framework: two boxes

Think Box — Behind the ball

All analytical and decision-making work happens here. You are a strategist in the Think Box.

  • Assess the lie, wind, elevation, and hazards.
  • Select your club and your specific target (not “the fairway” — a specific spot on the fairway).
  • Choose your shot shape if applicable.
  • Take your practice swing here, not at the ball.
  • See and feel the shot in your mind before stepping in.
  • Commit fully. Once you step across the line into the Play Box, no re-deciding.
Play Box — At address

All execution happens here. You are an athlete in the Play Box. No thinking — only trusting.

  • Step into your address position from behind the ball, aligned to your intermediate target.
  • Set your grip, feet, and posture.
  • Look at your target once. Look back at the ball.
  • Say your trigger word (choose one: “go,” “trust,” “smooth,” “swing”).
  • Swing. No waggles, no re-gripping, no second looks.

Your routine, step by step

1

Read the shot (Think Box begins)

Stand 4–6 feet behind the ball, on an extension of your target line. Assess: lie, wind, yardage to front/back, hazards. This should take no more than 15 seconds.

2

Commit to a specific target

Not “the green” — a specific section of the green, a specific spot in the fairway, a specific tree or flag in the distance. Pick an intermediate target (a divot or discoloration 3 feet in front of the ball, on your target line) to align to.

3

Visualize the shot

See the ball flight in your mind. Where does it start? Where does it land? See the whole shape. If you’re working a draw, see the ball starting right of target and curving back. 3–5 seconds. This primes the motor system for what’s coming.

4

Take your practice swing (still in Think Box)

One practice swing, behind the ball. Feel the swing you want to make. This is where your conscious swing thought goes — if you have one at all. From this point forward, no swing thoughts.

5

Cross the line — Play Box begins

Step into your address position, aligned to your intermediate target. This is the commitment point. You have made all decisions. You are done thinking. Do not re-evaluate from here.

6

Set up and look at the target once

Grip, stance width, ball position, posture. One look at your target. Then look back at the ball. Your eyes should finish on the ball (or just behind it).

7

Trigger word and swing

Say your trigger word silently. Then swing without hesitation. The trigger marks the point of no return — once you say it, you’re committed to the motion. Pick a word and stick with it across every shot this week.

Choosing your trigger word

The trigger word matters. It should be:

  • Simple: one syllable if possible. “Go.” “Now.” “Yes.” “Smooth.”
  • Neutral or positive: not a mechanical reminder (“spine angle” is a bad trigger word — it activates your analytical mind right before the swing).
  • Consistent: use the same word across every shot, every round, every practice session. This builds the association between the word and the committed state.

For reference, tour players typically use either a physical trigger (a forward press, a knee kick, a waggle sequence) or a mental cue word. Routines that are physically identical shot-to-shot are the most stable under pressure.

If you find yourself standing over the ball for more than 12–15 seconds, step back. You’ve entered Think Box mode while physically in the Play Box — which is the worst of both worlds. Step back, re-commit in the Think Box, and start the Play Box again.

When to use this routine

Every shot, every time, once you introduce it in Phase 3. This includes:

  • Every ball on the range in Phase 3 and 4 (not Phase 1 or 2, where the focus is mechanical).
  • Every shot during the Random 9-Shot Game and Course Simulation.
  • Every shot during drop-ball rounds.
  • Every shot on the course, forever, including putts.

The routine only works if it’s automatic. Automatic means thousands of repetitions. Start building those reps in Phase 3.

Handling discomfort over the ball

You mentioned not feeling comfortable standing over the ball. This is typically one of three things:

  1. Setup anxiety: uncertainty about aim, ball position, or grip. Solved by a consistent setup routine (intermediate target → feet → grip → posture). Build this as a checklist in Phase 1 and Phase 2 until it’s automatic.
  2. Commitment anxiety: you’re not fully decided on the shot before stepping in. The fix is to stay in the Think Box longer, not to rush through it. A fully committed swing you’re unsure about is worse than a slightly less optimal shot you’re certain about.
  3. Performance anxiety: concern about outcome. The Play Box model is specifically designed for this — once you cross the line, your job is to execute the swing, not to control the outcome. The outcome is the ball’s job.
The measure of a good pre-shot routine: not whether you hit the shot well, but whether you committed to it fully. A pull-hook you were fully committed to is a success from a routine perspective. A perfectly straight shot you were unsure about is a failure. Track commitment, not outcome, when you’re building the routine.
