Phase 1 — Posture & Setup Weeks 1–2

One job: feel what it means to maintain your spine angle through impact. Everything else is secondary. No driver this phase — the margin for error is too small to ingrain a new feel.

Primary drill: Wall Drill (also called the Glute-to-Wall Drill). 20 slow rehearsals before you hit a ball, every session. Build the proprioceptive map before asking your nervous system to reproduce it at full speed.

What success looks like at the end of Week 2

  • Low-point variance on 7-iron drops below 3 inches on the launch monitor.
  • You can describe in one sentence what “maintaining the spine angle” feels like from the inside.
  • Thin contact frequency with 7-iron is noticeably reduced (aim for <25% of balls).

Phase 2 — Clubface & Path Weeks 3–4

One job: square the clubface at impact by improving lead-wrist flexion in transition. Posture maintenance from Phase 1 is assumed — if it’s not solid yet, extend Phase 1 by a week.

Primary drills

  • Motorcycle Drill — lead-wrist flexion in the transition, the direct fix for the open-face pattern. 10 slow reps before every session.
  • Split-Grip Drill — exaggerates lead-wrist bow so you can feel what a square face requires. 20 balls per session on the range.
  • Gate Drill — introduces path awareness by setting tees to force an in-to-out swing direction. Start from 7-iron, move to 5-iron in Week 4.

What success looks like at end of Week 4

  • Face-to-path within ±3° on 7-iron (measured, not estimated).
  • Driver contact improves — fewer high-right misses off the face.
  • You can feel the difference between a cupped and a bowed lead wrist at impact.

Phase 3 — Variability & Routine Weeks 5–6

One job: introduce variability practice modes (differential, contextual interference) and install the Vision54 Think Box / Play Box pre-shot routine. The mechanics are learned; now train the nervous system to reproduce them under decision-making load.

Session structure

  • Range Day 1: Differential practice. Alternate between exaggerating the correction (heavy bow) and the old pattern (heavy cup). Forces the system to find the middle. 50 balls, never two identical swings in a row.
  • Range Day 2: Contextual interference. Rotate three clubs (8i, 6i, hybrid) with three different targets. Never repeat a club-target combination consecutively. Score 1 point per shot that starts on the intended side of the target.
  • Course/Transfer Day: Play 9 holes solo. Drop a second ball on every tee shot and every approach inside 175 yards. Log fairways hit, GIR, and miss tendency. Practice the pre-shot routine on every shot, both balls.

The routine (introduced this phase)

See the Pre-Shot Routine page for the full Think Box / Play Box protocol. The short version: make the decision and commit in the Think Box (behind the ball), then step into the Play Box (address) and trigger your swing with a single cue word. No second-guessing after you enter the Play Box.

Phase 4 — Pressure & Transfer Weeks 7–8

One job: move the skill to the course. Every range session should feel like a course simulation. Reduce block practice to near zero.

Session structure

  • Course-simulation range (Day 1): Play a virtual round. Pick a specific hole from your home course for each shot. Tee shot → approach → chip. Full pre-shot routine on every ball. Score: did it start on the right side? Did it land in a reasonable zone?
  • Launch monitor (Day 2): Stress-test KPIs. Track driver face-to-path, 7-iron low-point, smash factor. Aim to match Week 4 numbers under time pressure (set a 45-second clock between shots).
  • On-course rounds (Days 3–4): Play real golf. Log fairways, GIR, scrambling, putts, and score. Compare to your pre-plan baseline.

What success looks like at end of Week 8

  • Score reliably in the 83–89 range on a course you’ve played before.
  • Fairways hit above 45% (up from wherever you started).
  • GIR above 25% (3 or more greens per 9).
  • You have a pre-shot routine you trust and use on every shot.
  • You know, by feel, what early extension feels like — and you have a drill to reset it when it creeps back.

After Week 8

The plan doesn’t end — it cycles. Return to Phase 1 drills whenever your misses cluster back toward thin/right. The wall drill is a lifetime maintenance drill, not a beginner drill. Most tour pros have some version of it in their warm-up.

The single most important variable: consistency of practice. Three quality 75-minute sessions per week will outperform six mediocre ones. Show up for the three days and do the work that's on the plan.
