How the plan is structured

Practice methodology: the four modes

Most amateurs ‘practice’ by hitting 50 7-irons in a row at the same target. That's one of four legitimate practice modes — and almost always the wrong one.

Adam Young's framework, which is built on the motor-learning literature (Schmidt & Lee; the 2024 systematic review by Czyż et al. in Scientific Reports), divides practice into four distinct modes. Each one trains a different thing. The plan uses all four, in the right order.

The four modes

ModeWhat it isWhat it builds
BlockSame club, same target, same shot, repeated.Encoding a new mechanical pattern. Best early when you have a specific feel to install. Doesn’t transfer to the course on its own.
VariabilitySame target, same club, but vary the shot — high, low, fade, draw, three-quarter swing.Builds skill at hitting the shot you want in different conditions. Develops adaptability.
DifferentialPractice the opposite of what you want — try to hit the toe, hit the heel, hit a 50-yard slice, hit a 50-yard hook.Counter-intuitively powerful. By exploring the extremes, you train the calibration system. Adam Young’s research showed the differential group out-performed the centered-strike group on retention.
Calibrational / RandomDifferent target, different club, different shot every ball. Full pre-shot routine on every shot.The mode that transfers to the course. Feels worst on the range — that’s the point. The 2024 Scientific Reports meta-analysis confirmed: high contextual interference produces superior retention vs. blocked.

The progression

There’s a critical caveat to the random-is-better story. Novices benefit more from blocked practice early on, then need to shift toward random as their skill grows. You’re not a novice — you have a few years in — but you are encoding a new movement pattern (real hip rotation without early extension), so the early phases are heavier on blocked work. As the pattern gets reliable, the mix shifts.

PhasePractice mix
Phase 1 (Wk 1–2)85% block / 10% variability / 5% differential. You’re encoding a brand-new posture-maintenance feel. Variability now would just reinforce the old pattern.
Phase 2 (Wk 3–4)60% block / 20% variability / 10% differential / 10% calibrational. Path and face drills layered on top of posture. Add the differential ‘exaggerate the slice / hook’ set as a calibration tool.
Phase 3 (Wk 5–6)30% block / 25% variability / 15% differential / 30% calibrational. Half-and-half between technical and ‘play-the-range.’ Pre-shot routine on every calibrational shot.
Phase 4 (Wk 7–8)15% block / 15% variability / 10% differential / 60% calibrational. You’re training course transfer. Block becomes a short re-set before random play.

Three rules that apply every session

  1. Slow before fast. Every drill starts at 50% speed. Speed locks in whatever pattern you currently have, including the bad one.
  2. One feel at a time. If you have more than one swing thought, you have zero.
  3. End on a good shot. Don’t grind through 30 misses. If three drill reps fall apart, walk away from the bay for 60 seconds and reset.
