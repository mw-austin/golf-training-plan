Phase 1 · Posture & Setup
Why you're missing where you're missing

Diagnosis

Slice/push off the tee, thin contact with long clubs — the most common pattern in amateur golf, with a well-documented mechanical signature.

What the data says

Two large research efforts have done most of the heavy lifting on quantifying what separates pros from amateurs in this exact pattern.

GOLFTEC’s SwingTRU Motion Study

GOLFTEC has captured motion data on more than 90 million swings (more than 225 terabytes) across six million lessons. The SwingTRU study analyzed 48 body positions and identified six that correlate most strongly with handicap. Two of those six map directly onto your miss pattern.

Position (at impact)ProsHigh-handicapWhat it means for you
Hip turn at impact (open to target)36°19.5°Pros have nearly 2× the hip rotation through impact. Your recent hip-rotation focus is correct.
Shoulder bend at finish (forward tilt held through follow-through)32°3.2°Pros maintain forward tilt deep into the finish; high-handicappers stand up. This is early extension showing up at the end of the swing.

That 10× gap on shoulder bend at finish (32° vs. 3.2°) is the single biggest swing-shape difference between pros and high-handicaps in the SwingTRU dataset. It’s a near-perfect description of standing up through impact — which is what produces thin contact and an open face.

AMG — Athletic Motion Golf (3D motion capture)

AMG (Shaun Webb and Mike Granato — both ranked in Golf Digest’s Top 50 and Golf Magazine’s Top 100 instructors) pioneered the use of GEARS 3D motion capture and force-plate data in coaching. Their findings on early extension are counter-intuitive: tour pros also extend their pelvis through impact — they just do it in a fundamentally different way and from a different starting position. Tour pros set up with deeper pelvic tilt and more side-bend, then their extension happens late and rotationally. Amateurs extend early and toward the ball, which is what causes the pelvis to thrust forward and the body to stand up.

Practical implication: “fixing early extension” is not about preventing all extension. It’s about (a) starting from the right setup posture and (b) preserving the gap between your pelvis and the ball until late in the downswing. The wall drill teaches exactly this.

HackMotion’s wrist-data findings

HackMotion has analyzed more than one million tracked swings. Two findings matter for you. First, amateurs extend (cup) the lead wrist about 0.07 seconds before impact, compared to roughly 0.02 seconds for tour players. Second, a bowed lead wrist at impact correlates with both more distance and tighter accuracy. A cupped lead wrist at impact = an open clubface = the slice. The Motorcycle Drill (Phase 2) is the direct fix.

The mechanical story (your miss in plain English)

Three things are almost certainly happening together. They reinforce each other, which is why a single swing thought rarely fixes the pattern in isolation.

1. Early extension

Your pelvis thrusts toward the ball during the downswing instead of rotating around a stable axis. To make room for the club, you stand up. The stand-up is what produces the thin/topped contact, and it tilts your spine away from the target, which is the easiest way to leave the clubface open at impact.

Your recent focus on hip rotation is correct in principle. The risk is that “rotating” can be confused with “thrusting forward,” which is exactly what early extension is. The wall drill is built to help you feel the difference.

2. Open clubface at impact

A cupped lead wrist at impact = an open clubface = a slice. This is the direct cause of your right miss with the driver. Combined with even a slightly out-to-in path, you get the start-right or curve-right pattern.

3. Out-to-in (over-the-top) path

When the upper body initiates the downswing instead of the lower body, the club gets thrown out away from the body and then cuts across the ball from out to in. With an open face this produces a slice; with a square face, a pull. With an exaggerated stand-up move at the same time, you get the thin pull-slice off the deck.

The non-mechanical story

You said you don’t feel comfortable standing over the ball. That’s a real performance-execution issue, not a swing issue, and it has its own fix path — primarily a consistent pre-shot routine and a simple commit-and-trigger sequence. We build that in Phase 3.

What this means for the plan

  • Posture maintenance is the highest-leverage fix. The SwingTRU 32° vs. 3.2° finish-bend gap is the data backing this. Most early-stage range time goes to the wall drill and slow rehearsals.
  • Clubface control is second. HackMotion’s wrist data is the backing here. Built with split-grip and lead-wrist flexion drills, verified on the launch monitor.
  • Path correction follows naturally once you stop standing up and the face starts squaring. We don’t chase it directly until Phase 2.
  • Pre-shot comfort is built last. Mechanics + routine at the same time tends to fix neither.
