Wall Drill (Glute-to-Wall)
Phase 1 Posture Early extension

The highest-leverage drill in the plan. Teaches your body what it means to maintain spine angle and pelvis depth through impact — directly counteracting early extension.

How to do it: Stand a fist-width from a wall. Take your setup. As you swing through impact, your glutes should lightly brush or maintain proximity to the wall. If your pelvis thrusts forward (early extension), your glutes will move away. No ball required for slow rehearsals.

  • Start at 40–50% speed. Feel is everything here; speed comes later.
  • 20 rehearsal reps before hitting balls every session in Phase 1.
  • Use a mirror or phone video to confirm from the down-the-line view.
  • Once you can hit 7-irons correctly, move the wall drill to warm-up only.
Dosage
20 reps before every session
Club
No ball / 7-iron
Speed
40–70%

Pump Drill
Phase 1 Low point Spine angle

Builds awareness of forward shaft lean and low-point control. Helps you feel a correct impact position before committing to a full swing.

How to do it: Take a half-backswing, then pump the club down to impact position three times without hitting the ball. On the fourth pump, hit the ball. The three pumps let your nervous system rehearse the impact position at low speed.

  • Focus on keeping your weight forward on the lead foot during the pumps.
  • The club should be leaning toward the target (hands ahead of ball) at each pump stop.
  • After 10 pump-then-hit reps, do 10 free swings with no pump, maintaining the feel.
Dosage
10 pump-then-hit reps
Club
7-iron
When
After wall drill warm-up
Feet-Together Drill
Phase 1 Balance Low point

Placing your feet together forces you to swing in balance and eliminates the lateral sway that often enables early extension. Great diagnostic tool and corrective drill.

How to do it: Feet touching or 2 inches apart. Hit 7-irons at 60–70% speed. Any lateral sway or early extension will cause you to lose balance immediately — instant feedback.

  • If you can't make solid contact, slow down further before speeding up.
  • Focus on rotating around a stable center, not shifting toward the target.
  • 10 feet-together shots, then 10 normal shots. Alternate sets.
Dosage
2–3 sets of 10
Club
7-iron, 8-iron
Speed
60–70%
Slow-Motion Rehearsal
Phase 1 All phases Motor learning

Slow-motion rehearsal is not a drill so much as a practice method that belongs in every phase. Research on motor learning shows that explicit movement rehearsal at sub-30% speed helps encode new patterns before full-speed practice.

How to do it: Swing at roughly 10–20% speed, with or without a ball. Stop at impact and hold the position for 2 seconds. Then continue to the finish and hold. Check: are your glutes still near where they started? Is your head still?

Dosage
10 reps to open every session
Club
Any
Speed
10–25%
Motorcycle Drill
Phase 2 Clubface Lead wrist

The direct fix for an open clubface at impact. Teaches lead-wrist flexion (bowing) through the transition, which is the motor pattern that squares the face.

How to do it: At the top of your backswing, rotate your lead forearm as if you were revving a motorcycle throttle — toward you, not away. This bows the lead wrist and closes the face. Rehearse this motion without a ball 10 times before hitting. When hitting, feel this “rev” happen just as you start the downswing.

  • The move should happen at the very top / early transition, not at impact.
  • HackMotion sensor users: target 0° to −5° (bowed) lead wrist at impact.
  • Start with 7-iron at 70% speed. Slice/pull will reduce immediately if done correctly.
Dosage
10 rehearsals + 20 hit reps
Club
7-iron → driver

Split-Grip Drill
Phase 2 Clubface Lead wrist

Separating the hands 4–6 inches on the grip dramatically exaggerates feedback from the lead wrist. With a square or bowed lead wrist, the ball goes straight. With a cupped wrist, the face opens and the ball slices — instantly obvious.

How to do it: Grip the club normally with your lead hand. Slide your trail hand down the grip 4–6 inches, near the bottom. Hit 7-irons at 60–70% speed. The exaggerated feel makes face control obvious. After 15 split-grip shots, hit 15 normal-grip shots maintaining the feel.

Dosage
15 split + 15 normal
Club
7-iron
Speed
60–70%
Gate Drill
Phase 2 Path In-to-out

Sets up a physical gate with tees or alignment sticks to force an in-to-out swing path. Directly corrects the over-the-top move that contributes to the pull-slice pattern.

How to do it: Place two tees in the ground, one inside the ball line (6 inches behind impact, angled inward) and one outside the ball line (6 inches in front, angled outward). The gate forces the club to approach from inside and exit to the right of the target. Hit 7-irons, then 5-irons, working to avoid hitting either tee.

  • Start with a wider gate (easier) and narrow it as you improve.
  • Combine with the Motorcycle Drill: in-to-out path + closed face = a draw, not a slice.
Dosage
20 balls per session
Club
7-iron → 5-iron
Setup
2 tees or alignment sticks
Impact Bag / Towel Roll
Phase 2 Clubface Impact position

Hitting into an impact bag (or a rolled-up range towel placed on the ground) gives immediate physical feedback on impact position. You can feel whether the handle is leading, whether the face is square, and whether you’re extending through impact.

How to do it: Place an impact bag or firm towel roll just in front of where the ball would be. Swing through impact into the bag at 40–50% speed. At impact, check: are your hands ahead of the bag? Is the face square? Are you compressing, or scooping?

Dosage
15 reps, any session
Club
7-iron
Speed
40–50% (no ball)
Differential Practice Sets
Phase 3 Variability Motor learning

One of Adam Young’s four practice modes. Alternating between an exaggerated correction and the old fault teaches your nervous system to find the middle ground. More effective for retention than pure block practice.

How to do it: Hit one ball with a heavily exaggerated bowed lead wrist (overcorrection). Hit the next ball with a slightly cupped wrist (the old pattern). Alternate for 20 balls. Your system learns to calibrate between the two extremes and finds the optimal midpoint.

  • Don’t try to “find the middle” intentionally — let the nervous system do it.
  • You can apply this to any variable: extreme in-to-out path vs. out-to-in, etc.
Dosage
20 alternating balls
Club
7-iron → mix

Random 9-Shot Game
Phase 3 Transfer Decision-making

A structured variability game that forces decision-making between every shot and prevents the autopilot mode of blocked range practice. Maximizes contextual interference — one of the best-supported variables in motor learning research for long-term retention.

How to do it: Choose 3 clubs (e.g., 8i, 6i, hybrid) and 3 targets. You have 9 shot combinations (3 clubs × 3 targets). Randomize the order — never hit the same club-target combination twice in a row. Use your full pre-shot routine on every ball. Score 1 point per shot that starts on the intended side of the target. Aim for 7+/9.

Dosage
9 shots per round, 2–3 rounds
Club
3 clubs, rotating
Pre-shot routine
Required on every ball
Window Drill (Swing Shape)
Phase 3 Path Calibrational

A calibrational practice drill where you intentionally try to hit the ball through different imaginary “windows” — left window, center window, right window — by changing your swing shape. Builds awareness of how path and face interact to produce different shot shapes.

How to do it: Imagine three vertical windows, each about 15 yards apart, at your target distance. Hit 3 balls through the left window (draw/hook shape), 3 balls through the center, and 3 balls through the right window (fade/push shape). This forces you to intentionally adjust face and path, building shot-shaping awareness.

Dosage
9 balls (3 per window)
Club
7-iron or 6-iron
Course Simulation
Phase 4 Transfer Pressure

Play a virtual round on the range. Pick a specific hole from your home course for each shot. Play the entire hole: tee shot, then approach, then chip if needed. Full pre-shot routine required on every ball. Log whether each shot achieved its objective.

How to do it: Before heading to the range, write down 9 holes you want to play. For each hole, select the appropriate club and target (aim for a specific section of the range, not just a general direction). Play each hole mentally, then execute. This forces commitment on every shot.

  • Score: 1 point per shot that starts in the intended zone.
  • Log your “round score” over 8 weeks to track mental execution improvement.
Dosage
9 holes per session
Club
All clubs
Pre-shot routine
Required, every ball
Clock Pressure Game
Phase 4 Pressure Routine

Adds a time constraint to replicate the mild time pressure of real-golf decision-making. Rushing your pre-shot routine is one of the most common under-pressure failures.

How to do it: Set a 40-second timer between shots. You must complete your entire pre-shot routine and execute your swing before the timer goes off. If you’re rushed, you’ll feel it. The goal is a smooth, complete routine within the window — not fast, just efficient.

Dosage
Full session with timer
Club
All clubs
Timer
40 seconds between shots
Drop-Ball Round
Phase 4 On course Transfer

The single most effective transfer drill. Play 9 holes solo, dropping a second ball on every tee shot and every approach inside 175 yards. Play both balls, with your full pre-shot routine on each. Log the results for each ball.

  • Why it works: You get double the transfer reps on your highest-leverage shots, in a real course environment, under real lie and wind conditions — none of which the range provides.
  • Log: fairways hit (Ball 1 vs. Ball 2), GIR, miss direction, pre-shot routine execution rating (1–5).
  • Ignore score on the round. This is a measurement session, not a competition.
Dosage
9 holes, 2 balls on key shots
Location
Golf course (solo round)
Log
Fairways, GIR, misses
3-6-9 Putting Game
Short game All phases Putting

A pressure putting game for the bonus/short-game day. Make 3 putts in a row from 3 feet, then from 6 feet, then from 9 feet. If you miss at any stage, restart at 3 feet. Use your full pre-shot routine on each putt.

  • The restart rule creates pressure equivalent to a competitive round — without any head-to-head competition.
  • Track your “completion rate” over 8 weeks (how many sessions do you finish all three stages).
  • Aim to complete all three stages within 20 minutes.
